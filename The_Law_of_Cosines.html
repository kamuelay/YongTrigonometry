Section 4.2 The Law of Cosines

The Fijian canoe Uto Ni Yalo is sailing from Fiji to Tonga. The navigators know Tonga is 412 NM from Fiji in the House Lā Malanai (\(16.81^{\circ}\)). After sailing for one day and traveling 120 NM, the navigator determines they have gone off course by 2 houses (\(22.5^{\circ}\)). How far are they from Tonga and what is the angle that they will need to turn in order to reach their destination?

This triangle, where we know two sides and the angle between them, is known as Side-Angle-Side (SAS). Unfortunately, the Law of Sines that we learned in Section 4.1 only applies to ASA, AAS, and SSA. In this section, we will learn how to solve SAS triangles as well as triangles where all three sides are known, called Side-Side-Side (SSS) triangles.

Subsection 4.2.1 Law of Cosines

Theorem 4.2.1. Law of Cosines.

Given a triangle with sides \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) \(c\) and opposite angles \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) \(C\text{,}\) respectively,

\begin{align*} a^2\amp =\amp b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A\\ b^2\amp =\amp a^2+c^2-2ac\cos B\\ c^2\amp =\amp a^2+b^2-2ab\cos C \end{align*}

Thus, in any triangle, the square of one side equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides, minus twice their product times the cosine of their included angle.

Note: If the triangle is a right triangle, say \(C=90^{\circ}\text{,}\) then since \(\cos90^{\circ}=0\text{,}\) the Law of Cosines simplifies into the Pythagorean Theorem: \(c^2=a^2+b^2\text{.}\)

Proof.

To prove the first part, we will place a triangle with \(A\) at the origin and \(C\) on the \(x\)-axis, as shown below.

Using right triangle trigonometry, the coordinate of \(C\) can be written as \((b\cos A,b\sin A)\text{.}\) Using the Pythagorean Theorem, we get

\begin{align*} a^2\amp =\amp (b\cos A-c)^2+(b\sin A)^2\\ \amp =\amp b^2\cos^2A-2bc\cos A+c^2+b^2\sin^2A\\ \amp =\amp b^2(\cos^2A+\sin^2A)+c^2-2bc\cos A\\ \amp =\amp b^2(1)+c^2-2bc\cos A\\ \amp =\amp b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A \end{align*}

where we used the identity \(\cos^2A+\sin^2=1\text{.}\) The other two parts of the Law of Cosines can be proved using a similar argument.

Example 4.2.2. Using Law of Cosines (SAS): Fiji to Tonga.

We are now ready to return to our example where Uto Ni Yalo is sailing to Tonga, described in the triangle below.

How far are they from Tonga and what is the angle that they will need to turn in order to reach their destination?

Solution.

The distance to Tonga can be found using the Law of Cosines

\begin{align*} a^2\amp =\amp b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A\\ \amp =\amp 412^2+120^2-2\cdot412\cdot120\cos22.5^{\circ}\\ \amp \approx\amp 92,790.79 \end{align*}

Taking the square root of both sides we get

\begin{equation*} a\approx\sqrt{92,790.79}\approx304.62 \end{equation*}

Thus they are approximately 305 NM from their destination.

To determine the angle needed to adjust their course, we will first find \(B\) and subtract that from \(180^{\circ}\text{.}\) We can use either the Law of Sines or the Law of Cosines. If we use the Law of Sines

\begin{equation*} \frac{\sin A}{a}=\frac{\sin B}{b} \end{equation*}

we know \(a\approx304.62\text{,}\) \(b=412\text{,}\) and \(A=22.5^{\circ}\text{.}\) Notice this is the case SSA for Law of Sines where we get two triangles. Since we already know what the triangle looks like, we want the measure for \(B\) that makes it obtuse. However, to avoid this ambiguous case, we can simply use the Law of Cosines

\begin{equation*} b^2=a^2+c^2-2ac\cos B \end{equation*}

Rearranging we have

\begin{equation*} \cos B=\frac{a^2+c^2-b^2}{2ac}\approx\frac{304.62^2+120^2-412^2}{2\cdot304.62\cdot120}\approx-0.8556 \end{equation*}

So,

\begin{equation*} B\approx\cos^{-1}(-0.8556)\approx148.83^{\circ} \end{equation*}

Thus the angle Uto Ni Yalo needs to turn to adjust its course is \(180^{\circ}-148.83^{\circ}=31.17^{\circ}\) to the left.

Example 4.2.3. Using Law of Cosines (SSS).

If the sides of a triangle are \(a=4\text{,}\) \(b=6\text{,}\) and \(c=3\text{,}\) find the angles.

Solution.

To find \(A\text{,}\) we begin by using the Law of Cosines:

\begin{equation*} a^2=b^2+c^2-2ab\cos A \end{equation*}

Isolating the term with angle \(A\) we get

\begin{equation*} \cos A=\frac{b^2+c^2-a^2}{2bc}=\frac{7^2+3^2-5^2}{2\cdot7\cdot3}=\frac{33}{42} \end{equation*}

Taking the inverse cosine we get

\begin{equation*} A=\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{33}{42}\right)\approx38.21^{\circ} \end{equation*}

To find \(B\text{,}\) we continue with the Law of Cosines to get:

\begin{equation*} \cos B=\frac{a^2+c^2-b^2}{2ac}=\frac{5^2+3^2-7^2}{2\cdot5\cdot3}=\frac{15}{30}=\frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}

Taking the inverse cosine we get

\begin{equation*} B=\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=120^{\circ} \end{equation*}

To find the last angle, \(C\text{,}\) we can use the fact that the angles in a triangle sum to \(180^{\circ}\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} C=180^{\circ}-A-B\approx180^{\circ}-38.21^{\circ}-120^{\circ}=21.79^{\circ} \end{equation*}

Subsection 4.2.2 Area of an oblique triangle

The Greek mathematician, Heron of Alexandria, derived a formula for the area of a triangle when all three sides are given. Although a more modern derivation for this formula involves the Law of Cosines, it is still known as Heron's Formula.

Theorem 4.2.4. Heron's Formula.

The area

\begin{equation*} \mbox{Area} =\sqrt{s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)} \end{equation*}

where \(s=\frac{1}{2}(a+b+c)\) is half the perimeter of the triangle, also known as the semiperimeter.

Example 4.2.5. Polynesian Triangle.

The Polynesian Triangle is an area in the Pacific Ocean consisting of over 1,000 islands. These islands are collectively known as Polynesia. The northern corner of the Polynesian Triangle is made up of the Hawaiian Islands, the southwestern corner is made up of Aotearoa, and the southeastern corner is made up with Rapa Nui. It is believed that the first inhabitants to the islands arrived at Ka Lae, Hawai`i; Hokianga, Aotearoa; and Anakena, Rapa Nui, respectively. The distance between Ka Lae, Hawai`i and Hokianga, Aotearoa is 4,250 miles, the distance between Hokianga, Aotearoa and Anakena, Rapa Nui is 4,500 miles, and the distance between Rapa Nui and Ka Lae, Hawai`i is 4,450 miles. What is the area of the Polynesian Triangle rounded to the nearest square mile?

Solution.

Using Heron's Formula, we begin by calculating the semiperimeter

\begin{equation*} s=\frac{1}{2}(4,250+4,500+4,450)=6,600 \end{equation*}

Then the area created by the Polynesian Triangle is

\begin{equation*} \mbox{Area} =\sqrt{6,600(6,600-4,250)(6,600-4,500)(6,600-4,450)}\approx8,368,253\mbox{mi} ^2 \end{equation*}
