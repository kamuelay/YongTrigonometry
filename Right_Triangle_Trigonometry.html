Section 1.4 Right Triangle Trigonometry

During a voyage, a navigator uses a reference course to keep track of their position. This reference course is a line that runs between the starting point and the destination. If the wa‘a (canoe) encounters winds that push it from its intended course, the navigator can then mentally plot their position in relation to the reference course. To ensure they don’t miss their destination, navigators must keep track of how far they deviate from the intended course. They can do this by first measuring the angle of deviation from the intended reference course (in units of houses) and calculating the distance traveled. In this section, we will learn how to calculate the trigonometric functions with right triangles, which will allow us to determine how far a wa‘a has deviated from it’s intended reference course.

Subsection 1.4.1 Trigonometric Ratios

\begin{mybox}[Trigonometric Ratios] Consider a right triangle with \(\theta\) as one of its acute angles. The trigonometric ratios are defined as follows:

\begin{align*} \sin\theta\amp =\frac{\mbox{opposite} }{\mbox{hypotenuse} } \amp \cos\theta\amp =\frac{\mbox{adjacent} }{\mbox{hypotenuse} } \amp \tan\theta\amp =\frac{\mbox{opposite} }{\mbox{adjacent} }\\ \csc\theta\amp =\frac{\mbox{hypotenuse} }{\mbox{opposite} } \amp \sec\theta\amp =\frac{\mbox{hypotenuse} }{\mbox{adjacent} } \amp \cot\theta\amp =\frac{\mbox{adjacent} }{\mbox{opposite} } \end{align*}

A common mnemonic for remembering these relationships is SOHCAHTOA, formed from the first letters of “Sine is Opposite over Hypotenuse, Cosine is Adjacent over Hypotenuse, Tangent is Opposite over Adjacent.” \end{mybox}

Based on the definition of the six trigonometric functions, we have the following trigonometric identities. \begin{mybox}[Reciprocal Identities]

\begin{align*} \sin\theta\amp =\dfrac{1}{\csc\theta} \amp \cos\theta\amp =\dfrac{1}{\sec\theta} \amp \tan\theta\amp =\dfrac{1}{\cot\theta}\\ \csc\theta\amp =\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta} \amp \sec\theta\amp =\dfrac{1}{\cos\theta} \amp \cot\theta\amp =\dfrac{1}{\tan\theta} \end{align*}

\end{mybox}

\begin{mybox}[Quotient Identities]

\begin{align*} \tan\theta \amp =\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} \amp \cot\theta\amp =\dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} \end{align*}

\end{mybox}

Example 1.4.1.

Find the exact values of the six trigonometric ratios of the angle \(\theta\) in the given triangle.

Solution.

By the definition of the trigonometric ratios, we have

\begin{align*} \sin\theta\amp =\frac{3}{5} \amp \cos\theta\amp =\frac{4}{5} \amp \tan\theta\amp =\frac{3}{4}\\ \csc\theta\amp =\frac{5}{3} \amp \sec\theta\amp =\frac{5}{4} \amp \cot\theta\amp =\frac{4}{3} \end{align*}

Example 1.4.2.

Find the exact values of the six trigonometric ratios of the angle \(\theta\) in the given triangle.

Solution.

Notice that \(\theta\) is in a different position. Here, the adjacent side is 3 and the opposite side is 5. If we let \(h\) denote the hypotenuse, then we can use the Pythagorean Theorem to get

\begin{equation*} h=\sqrt{5^2+2^2}=\sqrt{29} \end{equation*}

Then by the definition of the trigonometric ratios, we have

\begin{align*} \sin\theta\amp =\frac{5}{\sqrt{29}}=\frac{5\sqrt{29}}{29} \amp \cos\theta\amp =\frac{2}{\sqrt{29}}=\frac{2\sqrt{29}}{29} \amp \tan\theta\amp =\frac{5}{2}\\ \csc\theta\amp =\frac{\sqrt{29}}{5} \amp \sec\theta\amp =\frac{\sqrt{29}}{2} \amp \cot\theta\amp =\frac{2}{5} \end{align*}

Subsection 1.4.3 Cofunctions

You may recognize the symmetry of \(\sin\theta\) and \(\cos\theta\) if reverse order of the values of sine and cosine from \(90^{\circ}\) to \(0^{\circ}\text{.}\) This symmetry gives us \(\sin0^{\circ}=\cos90^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(\sin30^{\circ}=\cos60^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(\sin45^{\circ}=\cos45^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(\sin60^{\circ}=\cos30^{\circ}\text{,}\) and \(\sin90^{\circ}=\cos0^{\circ}\text{.}\)

This pattern between sine and cosine is no coincidence. The three angles in a triangle sum to \(180^{\circ}\) or \(\pi\) radians. If we look at a right triangle, the two remaining angles will sum to \(90^{\circ}\) or \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) radians, meaning they are complementary angles.

Consider the right triangle in [cross-reference to target(s) "fig-cofunction" missing or not unique], where angles \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are complementary angles, side \(a\) is opposite of angle \(\alpha\text{,}\) and side \(b\) is opposite of angle \(\beta\text{.}\) Notice that we can also describe side \(b\) as adjacent to angle \(\alpha\) and side \(a\) as adjacent to angle \(\beta\text{.}\) Therefore,

\begin{equation*} \sin\alpha=\dfrac{\mbox{opposite} }{\mbox{hypotenuse} }=\dfrac{a}{c}\mbox{and} \cos\beta=\dfrac{\mbox{adjacent} }{\mbox{hypotenuse} }=\dfrac{a}{c} \end{equation*}

Thus we can conclude that

\begin{equation*} \sin\alpha=\frac{a}{c}=\cos\beta \end{equation*}

} Sine and cosine are called cofunctions because of this relationship between these functions and their complementary angles. We can obtain similar relationships for all trigonometric functions:

\begin{align*} \sin\alpha=\frac{a}{c}\amp =\cos\beta \amp \cos\alpha=\frac{b}{c}\amp =\sin\beta \amp \tan\alpha=\frac{a}{b}\amp =\cot\beta\\ \csc\alpha=\frac{c}{a}\amp =\sec\beta \amp \sec\alpha=\frac{c}{b}\amp =\csc\beta \amp \cot\alpha=\frac{b}{a}\amp =\tan\beta \end{align*}

Since \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are complementary angles, \(\alpha+\beta=90^{\circ}\text{.}\) Rearranging, we get \(\beta=90^{\circ}-\alpha\text{.}\) Substituting this into our cofunctions and replacing \(\alpha\) with \(\theta\text{,}\) we get our cofunction identities.

\begin{mybox}[Cofunction Identities] The cofunction identities in degrees are

\begin{align*} \sin\theta\amp =\cos(90^{\circ}-\theta) \amp \cos\theta\amp =\sin(90^{\circ}-\theta) \amp \tan\theta\amp =\cot(90^{\circ}-\theta)\\ \csc\theta\amp =\sec(90^{\circ}-\theta) \amp \sec\theta\amp =\csc(90^{\circ}-\theta) \amp \cot\theta\amp =\tan(90^{\circ}-\theta) \end{align*}

The cofunction identities in radians are

\begin{align*} \sin\theta\amp =\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right) \amp \cos\theta\amp =\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right) \amp \tan\theta\amp =\cot\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right)\\ \csc\theta\amp =\sec\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right) \amp \sec\theta\amp =\csc\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right) \amp \cot\theta\amp =\tan\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\theta\right) \end{align*}

\end{mybox}

Subsection 1.4.4 Using a Calculator

Sometimes you may encounter an angle other than the special angles described above. In this case, you will have to use a calculator.

First, be sure that your angle is either in degrees or radians, depending on the problem, refer to your calculator's manual for instructions. Most calculators will have a special button for the sine, cosine, and tangent functions. Depending on your calculator, you may see the following keys for

To calculate cosecant, secant, and cotangent, you will need to use the identity

\begin{align*} \csc\theta \amp = \frac{1}{\sin\theta}, \amp \sec\theta \amp = \frac{1}{\cos\theta}, \amp \cot\theta \amp = \frac{1}{\tan\theta} \end{align*}

Answers produced by calculators are estimates and you should pay close attention to see if the question is asking for the exact solution or a decimal approximation. For example, if you need to calculate \(\sin45^{\circ}=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\text{,}\) the calculator may give the answer as \(\sin45^{\circ}\approx0.70710678\text{,}\) which is a decimal approximation since the actual solution goes on forever. Unless stated otherwise, answers in the book should be exact, e.g. \(\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\) and not 0.70710678.

Example 1.4.7.

Use a calculator to evaluate

  1. \(\displaystyle \sin22^{\circ}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \cos5^{\circ}\)

  3. \(\displaystyle \cot53^{\circ}\)

  4. \(\displaystyle \cos5\mbox{ rad}\)

Solution.

We make sure our calculator is in degree or radian mode. For simplicity, we will round our answers to four decimals.

  1. Input: SIN(22); Output: \(0.3746\)

  2. Input: COS(5); Output: \(0.9962\)

  3. Since most calculators do not have a key for cotangent, we Input: (1/TAN(53)); Output: \(\dfrac{1}{1.3270}\approx0.7536\)

  4. Since this problem uses radians, we must change the mode on our calculator then Input: COS(5); Output: \(0.2837\text{.}\)

Notice that \(\cos5^{\circ}\neq\cos5\mbox{ rad}\text{.}\) This is why it is important to double check if your calculator is on degree or radian mode.

Subsection 1.4.5 Solving Right Triangles

Consider the following right triangle where side \(a\) is opposite angle \(\alpha\text{,}\) side \(b\) is opposite angle \(\beta\text{,}\) and side \(c\) is the hypotenuse. Since \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are complementary angles, we have

\begin{equation*} \alpha+\beta=90^{\circ} \end{equation*}

Additionally, by the Pythagorean Theorem, we have

\begin{equation*} a^2+b^2=c^2 \end{equation*}

Definition 1.4.9.

To solve a triangle means to determine the values for all three lengths of its sides and the measures of all three angles given some information about the triangle.

\begin{mybox}[Solving Right Triangles] To solve a right triangle, it is useful to use the following

\begin{equation*} \alpha+\beta=90^{\circ}, \qquad a^2+b^2=c^2 \end{equation*}

\end{mybox}

Example 1.4.10.

Solve the right triangle. Round your answer to two decimal places.

Solution.

Since this is a right triangle, we know one angle is \(90^{\circ}\text{,}\) we also know that the other angle is \(50^{\circ}\) and the adjacent side is 16. To solve this triangle, we will need to determine the values of \(a\text{,}\) \(c\text{,}\) and \(\beta\text{.}\) We begin by finding \(\beta\text{.}\) Since \(50^{\circ}+\beta=90^{\circ}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \beta=90^{\circ}-50^{\circ}=40^{\circ} \end{equation*}

Next, we will solve for \(a\text{.}\) If we use the angle \(50^{\circ}\text{,}\) the adjacent side is 16 and \(a\) is the opposite side to the angle. The trigonometric function that relates these is tangent:

\begin{equation*} \tan50^{\circ}=\frac{a}{16} \end{equation*}

multiplying both sides by 16 we get

\begin{equation*} a=16\cdot\tan50^{\circ}\approx19.07 \end{equation*}

Using the Pythagorean Theorem, we get

\begin{equation*} c^2=16^2+19.07^2\approx619.66 \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} c\approx\sqrt{619.66}\approx24.89 \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.4.6 Solving Applied Problems

Example 1.4.11. Deviation.

We are now ready to calculate the deviation example proposed at the start of this section. In an average day of sailing, a waʻa sails for 120 nautical miles (NM). If Hikianalia is supposed to sail in the direction of Hikina (East), but currents have deviated her course by one house so she actually sailed toward the house Lā, how far off the course has Hōkūleʻa deviated?

Solution.

From the Star Compass (Figure 1.1.1), the house Lā is one house (\(11.25^{\circ}\)) from Hikina. If we let \(y\) denote the distance deviated from the reference course, our right triangle becomes:

Since we know the hypotenuse of the triangle and want to find the side opposite of the angle, we will use the sine function:

\begin{equation*} \sin11.25^{\circ}=\frac{\mbox{opposite} }{\mbox{hypotenuse} }=\frac{y}{120\mbox{ NM} } \end{equation*}

multiplying both sides by 120, we get

\begin{equation*} y=120\cdot\sin11.25^{\circ}\mbox{ NM} \approx23.4\mbox{ NM} \end{equation*}

So Hikianalia has deviated 23.4 nautical miles north from the reference course.

Example 1.4.12. Solar panels.

Solar panels use the sun's energy to produce electricity. In order to maximize the energy output, solar panels should be installed perpendicular to the sun's light. The angle of elevation of the sun varies by latitude and in Hawai‘i, south facing solar panels should have a pitch of \(21^{\circ}\) to be perpendicular to the sun’s rays. When installing a solar panel, it may be difficult to determine the pitch, so instead of measure the angle, you can measure the height of the top of the panel. What height should a south facing solar panel that is 65 in in length be installed to achieve the optimal angle of \(21^{\circ}\text{?}\) Round your answer to the nearest tenth of an inch.

Solution.

We begin by drawing the triangle.

Since we know the desired angle of pitch of the solar panel and the length of the panel, we can set up the following equation

\begin{align*} \sin21^{\circ}\amp =\amp \frac{\mbox{opposite} }{\mbox{hypotenuse} }=\frac{\mbox{height} }{65\mbox{ in} }\\ \mbox{height} \amp =\amp 65\mbox{ in} \cdot\sin21^{\circ}\approx23.3\mbox{ in} \end{align*}

So when installing the solar panel in Hawai`i, the top of the solar should should be 23.3 inches above the bottom in order to maximize the energy output.
