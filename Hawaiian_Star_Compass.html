Section 1.1 Hawaiian Star Compass

Hawaii ʻ This is a test. Pacific Islanders have been wayfinding across long distances in the Pacific long before the magnetic compasses or other instruments were introduced. Navigators relied on observations of the stars, sun, ocean, winds, and other signs from nature to determine their position. Through time, much of the art and science of traditional wayfinding was lost across the Pacific, except with a few navigators. The island of Satawal in the Federated States of Micronesia was home to Mau Piailug, one of the last traditional navigators. To determine his position, Mau used the paafu, a Micronesian Star Compass which is mental construct used to help determine position by memorizing things important to navigation, such as the position of stars when they rise and set on the horizon. The star compass also reads the flight path of birds and the direction of winds and waves.

Wanting to revive wayfinding in Hawai‘i, Nainoa Thompson traveled to the island of Satawal to learn from Mau Piailug. Thompson adopted Mau’s star compass, resulting in the Hawaiian Star Compass (Figure 1.1.1).

In the star compass, Thompson divides the visual horizon into 32 equidistant points around a circle, called houses. Houses represent a space on the horizon where a celestial bodies such as the sun, stars, moon, and planets rise and set. In the same way that we have addresses to help people find our homes, each celestial body has its own address represented with these houses.

The four cardinal points correspond to a house. The stars arrive from the horizon called Hikina ( “To Arrive” ) or East and enter the horizon called Komohana ( “To Enter” ) or West. If you stand with your back towards Hikina (East) and face Komohana (West), your right will point towards ʻĀkau ( “Right” ) or North and your left will point towards Hema ( “Left” ) or South.

These four cardinal points divide the compass into four quadrants. Each quadrant is named after winds in Hawai`i. Koʻolau is the Northeast quadrant named for the trade winds; Hoʻolua is the Northwest quadrant, Kona is the Southwest quadrant; and Malanai is the Southeast quadrant.

Each house on the compass has a name. The corresponding houses in the east and in the west share a name. Starting in the east or west and moving northwards and southwards is the first house positioned on either side of Hikina (East) and Komohana (West). This is the house Lā (Sun). It is followed by ʻĀina (Land), Noio (Tern), Manu (Bird), Nālani (Heavens), Nā Leo (Voices), and Haka (Empty). The 32 houses in the Hawaiian Star Compass correspond to the points of the 32-wind compass rose (Table 1.1.2). The celestial bodies move in parallel paths across the sky from East to West, rising and setting in the same house, remaining in its hemisphere (Figure 1.1.3).

For example, if a star arrives in the Koʻolau (northeastern) quadrant in the star house Noio, it will arc overhead, staying in the northern hemisphere and enters the horizon in the same house it arrived in, Noio, but in the Hoʻolua (northwest) quadrant. If a star arrives in the Malanai (southeast) quadrant in the house Lā, it will remain in the southern hemisphere as it arcs overhead and enters the horizon in the house Lā in the Kona (southwestern) quadrant.

The star compass is also used to determine direction based on wind and ocean swells. As the wind and swells move, they cross the star compass diagonally. If a wind is blowing from the house `āina in the Koʻolau (northeast) quadrant, it would blow in the direction of the Kona (southwest) quadrant and exit at the same house it originated from, `āina.

Now that we know the star compass, you may be asking how do we tell direction? To do this, we use observations. At night, Thompson uses about 220 stars to tell direction, memorizing where they rise and set on horizon. During the day when the sun is low on the horizon, we can use the sun but as it gets higher, you no longer are able to tell where on the horizon it came from. You can also memorize where the wind and waves are coming from and check to see if it has changed direction between sunrise and sunset.

In addition to the star compass, many cultures across the Pacific use a wind compass. Similar to the star compass, the wind compass is also a mental construct.
