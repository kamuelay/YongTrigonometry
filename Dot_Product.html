A voyage undertaken using modern wayfinding must design a course strategy, which includes a reference course for reaching the vicinity of one's destination, hopefully upwind, so that the canoe can sail downwind to the destination rather than having to tack into the wind to get there. (Tacking involves sailing back and forth as closely as possible into the wind to make progress against the wind; it's very arduous and time-consuming, something to be avoided if at all possible, particularly at the end of a long, difficult voyage.)

Subsection 4.4.1 The Dot Product of Two Vectors

Definition 4.4.4.

If \(\mathbf{u}=\langle u_1,u_2\rangle\) and \(\mathbf{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle\) are vectors, then their dot product, written \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}\) is defined as

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=u_1v_1+u_2v_2 \end{equation*}

In other words, we compute the dot product by multiplying the corresponding components of the two vectors and adding the results. Note: The dot product is not a vector, but instead is a real number (a scalar)

Example 4.4.5. Finding Dot Products.

Here we calculate some dot products. Maybe we can calculate \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}\text{,}\) \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}\text{,}\) \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u}\text{,}\) \(\|\mathbf{u}\|\)

\begin{mybox}[Properties of the Dot Product] Let \(\mathbf{u}\text{,}\) \(\mathbf{v}\text{,}\) and \(\mathbf{w}\) be vectors and let \(c\) be a scalar (real number). Then

  1. \(\displaystyle \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle c(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})=(c\mathbf{u})\cdot\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{u}\cdot(c\mathbf{v})\)

  3. \(\displaystyle (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w}+\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w}\)

  4. \(\displaystyle \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u}=\|\mathbf{u}\|^2\)

  5. \(\displaystyle \mathbf{0}\cdot\mathbf{v}=0\)

The proof of many of these can be left as an exercise, but I should probably problem one or two. \end{mybox}
