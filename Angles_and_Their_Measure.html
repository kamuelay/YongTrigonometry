Section 1.2 Angles and Their Measure

One method people use to identify their position is by looking at the latitude. These imaginary lines that form circles around the Earth run parallel to the Equator. The latitude of a place is defined as the angle between a line drawn from the center of the earth to that point and the equatorial plane. For any point in the Northern Hemisphere, a navigator can measure their latitude by determining the angle Hōkūpaʻa (North Star) makes with the horizon.

When voyaging, knowing correct angles can be the difference between finding and missing your destination. Navigators pay close attention to angles on the Hawaiian Star Compass to determine where celestial bodies enter and exit the sky as well as where wind and current come from. In this section, we will examine the properties of angles and their measure.

Definition 1.2.1.

A ray is a part of a line that begins at a point \(O\) and extends in one direction.

Definition 1.2.2.

If two rays have a common vertex, \(O\text{,}\) they form an angle, \(\theta\text{.}\) The starting ray of the angle is called the initial side and the second ray is called the terminal side.

Subsection 1.2.1 Degree

The measure of an angle is the amount of rotation from the initial side to the terminal side. One unit of measuring angles is the degree. One degree is \(\frac{1}{360}\) of a complete revolution, so one full revolution is \(360^{\circ}\text{.}\)

Today we often use decimals to represent the fractional parts of a degree. However, fractional parts of a degree were traditionally represented in minutes and seconds. One minute or arc minute, denoted \(1'\text{,}\) is defined as \(\frac{1}{60}(1^{\circ})\) and one second or arc second, denoted \(1''\text{,}\) is defined as \(\frac{1}{60}(1')\text{.}\) \begin{mybox}[Conversion Between Degree, Minutes, and Seconds]

\begin{align*} 1^{\circ}\amp =60' \amp 1'\amp =\left(\frac{1}{60}\right)^{\circ} \amp 1''\amp =\left(\frac{1}{3600}\right)^{\circ}\\ 1^{\circ}\amp =3600'' \amp 1'\amp =60'' \amp 1''\amp =\left(\frac{1}{60}\right)' \end{align*}

\end{mybox}

Example 1.2.3.

In the Star Compass (Figure 1.2.4), the angle that divides the houses Manu (northwest) and Noio (northwest by west) is \(140.625^{\circ}\text{.}\) Represent \(140.625^{\circ}\) in degrees, minutes, and seconds.

Solution.

First we will convert \(0.625^{\circ}\) to minutes using the conversion \(1^{\circ}=60'\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} 0.625^{\circ}=0.625^{\circ}\cdot\frac{60'}{1^{\circ}}=37.5' \end{equation*}

Since \(1'=60''\text{,}\) we can convert \(0.5'\) to seconds: \(\displaystyle 0.5'=0.5'\cdot\frac{60''}{1'}=30''\text{.}\)

So \(140.625^{\circ}=140^{\circ}37'30''\text{.}\)

Example 1.2.5.

Convert \(263^{\circ}24'45''\) to decimal degrees.

Solution.

We will first convert \(24'\) and \(45''\) to degrees.

\begin{equation*} 24'=24\cdot1'=24\cdot\left(\frac{1}{60}\right)^{\circ}=0.4^{\circ} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} 45''=45\cdot1''=45\cdot\left(\frac{1}{3600}\right)^{\circ}=0.0125^{\circ} \end{equation*}

So \(263^{\circ}24'45''=263^{\circ}+24'+45''=263^{\circ}+0.4^{\circ}+0.0125=263.4125^{\circ}\)

Definition 1.2.6.

If an angle is drawn on the \(xy\)-plane and the vertex is at the origin and the initial side is on the positive \(x\)-axis, then that angle is said to be in standard position. If the angle is measured in a counterclockwise rotation, the angle is said to be a positive angle and if the angle is measured in a clockwise rotation, the angle is said to be a negative angle.

Definition 1.2.7.

When an angle is in standard position, the terminal side will either lie in a quadrant or it will lie on the \(x\)-axis or \(y\)-axis. An angle is called a quadrantal angle if the terminal side lies on \(x\)-axis or \(y\)-axis. The two axes divide the plane into four quadrants. In the Cartesian plane, the four quadrants are Quadrant I, II, III, and IV. The corresponding quadrants of Star Compass are Koʻolau (NE), Hoʻolua (NW), Kona (SE), and Malanai (SW).

Definition 1.2.8.

Coterminal angles are angles in standard position that have the same initial side and the same terminal side. Any angle has infinitely many coterminal angles because each time we add or subtract \(360^{\circ}\) from it, the resulting angle has the same terminal side.

Example 1.2.9.

\(90^{\circ}\) and \(450^{\circ}\) are coterminal angles since \(450^{\circ}-360^{\circ}=90^{\circ}\)

To determine the quadrant in which an angle lies, add or subtract one revolution (\(360^{\circ}\)) until you obtain a coterminal angle between \(0^{\circ}\) ad \(360^{\circ}\text{.}\) The quadrant that the terminal side lies is the quadrant of the angle. Quadrantal angles do not lie in any quadrant.

Example 1.2.10.

Determine the quadrant in which each angle lies and name the corresponding House and quadrant in the Star Compass (Figure 1.2.4).

  1. \(\displaystyle 140^{\circ}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle -770^{\circ}\)

  3. \(\displaystyle 923^{\circ}\)

Solution.

  1. Since \(90^{\circ}\lt 140^{\circ}\lt 180^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(140^{\circ}\) lies in Quadrant II, or Manu Hoʻolua.

  2. Since \(-770^{\circ}\lt 0\text{,}\) we first add \(3\times360\) to \(-770^{\circ}\) to obtain an angle \(0^{\circ}\) and \(360^{\circ}\text{,}\)

    \begin{equation*} -770^{\circ}+3\times360^{\circ}=310^{\circ} \end{equation*}

    So \(310^{\circ}\) and \(-770^{\circ}\) are coterminal. Since \(270^{\circ}\lt 310^{\circ}\lt 360^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(310^{\circ}\) lies in Quadrant IV, or Manu Malanai.

  3. Since \(923^{\circ}>360\) we begin by subtracting \(2\times360^{\circ}\)

    \begin{equation*} 923^{\circ}-2\times360^{\circ}=203^{\circ} \end{equation*}

    So \(203^{\circ}\) and \(923^{\circ }\) are coterminal. Since \(180^{\circ}\lt 203^{\circ}\lt 270^{\circ}\text{,}\) \(923^{\circ}\) lies in Quadrant III or ʻĀina Kona.

Example 1.2.11.

What is the corresponding quadrant for Nalani Kona?

Solution.

Locating Nalani Kona in the Star Compass, we see it is in Quadrant III.

Definition 1.2.12.

A central angle is a positive angle formed at the center of a circle by two radii.

Subsection 1.2.2 Radian

Another way to measure an angle is with radians which measure the the arc of a circle that is formed from an angle.

Definition 1.2.13. Definition of a Radian.

The radian measure of a central angle in a circle is the ratio of the length of the arc on a circle subtended by the angle to the radius. If \(r\) is the radius of the circle, \(\theta\) is the angle, and \(s\) is the arc length, then we have the following

\begin{equation*} \theta=\frac{s}{r} \end{equation*}

A radian is abbreviated by rad.

The measure of a central angle obtained when the length of the arc is also equal to the radius, \(r\text{,}\) is called one radian (1 rad). Similarly, if \(\theta=2\) rad, then the arc length equals \(2r\text{.}\)

The circumference of a circle is \(C=2\pi r\text{.}\) This means that the circumference is \(2\pi\approx6.28\) times the radius. This means that if we were to use a piece of string with the length of the radius, we would use six pieces of string plus a fractional piece of the string.

\begin{mybox}[Relationships Between Degrees and Radians] If a circle with radius 1 is drawn, there are \(360^{\circ}\) and the full arc length is the circumference, which is \(2\pi\text{.}\) Therefore, the relationship between degrees and radians is:

\begin{equation*} 360^{\circ}=2\pi \mbox{ radian} , \mbox{or } 180^{\circ}=\pi\mbox{ radian} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 1\mbox{ radian} =\frac{180^{\circ}}{\pi} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} 1^{\circ}=\frac{\pi}{180}\mbox{ radian} \end{equation*}

  1. To convert degree to radians, multiply by \(\dfrac{2\pi\mbox{ radians} }{360^{\circ}}\) or \(\dfrac{\pi\mbox{ radian} }{180^{\circ}}\)

  2. To convert radians to degrees, multiply by \(\dfrac{360^{\circ}}{2\pi\mbox{ radians} }\) or \(\dfrac{180^{\circ}}{\pi\mbox{ radian} }\)

\end{mybox}

Example 1.2.15.

Express \(45^{\circ}\) in radians.

Solution.

\(\displaystyle 45^{\circ}=45^{\circ}\left(\frac{2\pi\mbox{ radians} }{360^{\circ}}\right)=\frac{\pi}{4}\mbox{radians}\)

Example 1.2.16.

Express \(\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\) in degrees.

Solution.

\(\displaystyle \frac{5\pi}{6}\mbox{rad} =\frac{5\pi}{6}\mbox{rad} \left(\frac{360^{\circ}}{2\pi\mbox{ rad} }\right)=150^{\circ}\)

Subsection 1.2.3 Arc Length

Recall the definition of a radian is the ratio of the arc length to the radius of a circle, \(\theta=\frac{s}{r}\text{.}\) Rearranging this, we can obtain a formula for the arc length of a circle.

Theorem 1.2.18.

In a circle of radius \(r\text{,}\) the arc length, \(s\text{,}\) subtended by a central angle (in radians), \(\theta\text{,}\) is

\begin{equation*} s=r\theta \end{equation*}

If \(\theta\) is given in degrees, then \(\displaystyle s=2\pi r\cdot\left(\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\right)\text{.}\)

Example 1.2.19.

Find the length of an arc of a circle with radius 10 cm subtended by an angle of \(120^{\circ}\text{.}\)

Solution.

Using the arc formula we get \(\displaystyle s=2\pi 10\mbox{cm} \cdot\left(\frac{120^{\circ}}{360^{\circ}}\right)=\frac{20\pi}{3}\mbox{cm}\text{.}\)

Example 1.2.20.

Kiritimati, also known as Christmas Island, is an atoll in the Republic of Kiribati. Kiritimati's location west of the International Date Line makes it one of the first places in the world to welcome the New Year, while Hawai`i is one of the last places. Although Kiritimati and Moloka`i share the same longitude at \(157^{\circ}12'\) west (meaning Moloka`i is directly north of Kiritimati), both islands are 24 hours apart. For example, if the time on Oʻahu is 3:00 pm on Thursday, then at that same moment it is 3:00 pm on Friday in Kiritimati. Find the distance between Kiritimati (\(1^{\circ}45'\) north latitude) and Moloka`i (\(21^{\circ}08'\) north latitude). Assume the radius of Earth is 3,960 miles and that the central angle between the two islands is the difference in their laititudes.

Solution.

The measure of the central angle between the two islands is

\begin{align*} \theta\amp =\amp 21^{\circ}08'-1^{\circ}45'\\ \amp =\amp 19^{\circ}23'\\ \amp =\amp 19^{\circ}+\frac{23}{60}^{\circ}\\ \amp \approx\amp 19.3833^{\circ} \end{align*}

To find the distance, we use Theorem 1.2.18 to find the arc length:

\begin{equation*} s=2\pi r\cdot\left(\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\right)\approx2\pi\cdot(3,960\mbox{ miles} )\frac{19.3833^{\circ}}{360^{\circ}}\approx1,340\mbox{ miles} \end{equation*}

So the distance between Kiritimati and Moloka`i is approximately 1,340 miles.

Subsection 1.2.4 Area of a Sector of a Circle

\begin{mybox}[Area of a Sector] The area of the sector of a circle of radius \(r\) formed by a central angle of \(\theta\) is

\begin{align*} A\amp =\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\cdot\pi\cdot r^2,\mbox{ when \(\theta\) is in degrees}\\ A\amp =\frac{1}{2}r^2\theta,\mbox{ when \(\theta\) is in radians} \end{align*}

\end{mybox} Notice the ratio \(\displaystyle\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\) is the proportion of the angle \(\theta\) (in degrees) to one complete circle. Additionally, the circumference of a circle is given by \(2\pi r\text{.}\) Therefore, the arc length is simply the proportion of the central angle to the whole circle multiplied by the circumference of the circle.

\begin{equation*} s=\mbox{arc length} =\mbox{(proportion of circle)} \cdot\mbox{(circumference)} =\left(\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\right)\cdot(2\pi r) \end{equation*}

Similarly, the area of a circle is given by \(\pi r^2\text{.}\) So the area of sector is the proportion of the central angle to the whole circle multiplied by the area of the circle.

\begin{equation*} A=\mbox{area of sector} =\mbox{(proportion of circle)} \cdot\mbox{(area of circle)} =\left(\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\right)\cdot\left(\pi r^2\right) \end{equation*}

Theorem 1.2.21.

Given a circle of radius \(r\) formed by a central angle of \(\theta\text{,}\) then the arc length and area of the sector formed by \(\theta\) can be expressed as the proportion of the angle to the full circle multiplied by the circumference and area of the circle, respectively.

\begin{equation*} s=\mbox{(proportion of circle)} \cdot\mbox{(circumference)} =\left(\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\right)\cdot(2\pi r) \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} A=\mbox{(proportion of circle)} \cdot\mbox{(area of circle)} =\left(\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\right)\cdot\left(\pi r^2\right) \end{equation*}

Example 1.2.22.

When sailing, a waʻa (canoe) cannot make headway by sailing directly into the wind. It can only sail beyond \(67^{\circ}\) in either direction from the wind ([cross-reference to target(s) "fig_Sailing-upwind" missing or not unique]). If Hōkūleʻa sails for 50 miles, what is the area of the sector that cannot be sailed? Round your answer to the nearest square mile.

Solution.

The angle is \(\theta=2\cdot67^{\circ}=134^{\circ}\) and the radius is \(r=50\) miles. So the area is given by

\begin{equation*} A=\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}\pi\cdot r^2=\frac{134}{360}\pi\cdot 50^2\approx2,923\mbox{ square miles} \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.2.5 Angular Velocity and Linear Speed

Consider an object moving along a circle as shown on the right. There are two ways to describe the circular motion of this object: linear speed which measures the distance traveled; and angular speed which measures the rate at which the central angle changes.

Definition 1.2.24. Linear Speed.

Suppose an object moves along a circle with radius \(r\) and \(\theta\) (measured in radians) is the angle transversed in time \(t\text{.}\) Let \(s\) be the distance the object traveled in time \(t\text{.}\) Then the linear speed, \(v\text{,}\) of the object is given by

\begin{equation*} v=\frac{s}{t} \end{equation*}

Definition 1.2.25. Angular Speed.

Suppose an object moves along a circle. Let \(\theta\) (measured in radians) be the angle transversed by the object in time \(t\text{.}\) The angular speed, \(\omega\text{,}\) of the object is given by

\begin{equation*} \omega=\frac{\theta}{t} \end{equation*}

Notice that we can rearrange the angular speed to get \(\theta=\omega t\text{.}\) Since \(s\) is an arc length, we have \(s=r\theta\text{,}\) and thus we can write the linear speed as

\begin{equation*} v=\frac{s}{t}=\frac{r\theta}{t}=\frac{r\omega t}{t}=r\omega \end{equation*}

Definition 1.2.26. Linear Speed.

Suppose an object moves along a circle with radius \(r\) and an angular speed \(\omega\) (measured in radians per unit time). Then the linear speed, \(v\text{,}\) of the object is given by

\begin{equation*} v=r\omega \end{equation*}

Example 1.2.27. Une.

One method a waʻa uses to change direction is with the hoe uli, or the steering paddle. When a sharp turn is needed for maneuvers such as tacking, the steersperson will turn the handle of the hoe uli in a circular motion, as a lever to scoop the paddle in the water and change the heading of a vessel. This move is called une, which literally translates to “lever.” If the steerperson is performing an une at a rate of 25 rotations per minute and the radius of the circular movement is 2 feet, calculate

  1. the angular speed measured in radians per minute

  2. the linear speed of the hoe uli in miles per hour (round your answer to two decimal places)

Solution.

  1. We are given the angular speed is \(\omega=25\) revolutions per minute. To convert our angular speed to radians per minute, we use the fact that one revolution is \(2\pi\) radians to get

    \begin{equation*} \omega=25\frac{\mbox{revolution} }{\mbox{minute} }=25\frac{\mbox{rev} }{\mbox{minute} }\cdot2\pi\frac{\mbox{radians} }{\mbox{revolution} }=50\pi\frac{\mbox{radians} }{\mbox{minute} } \end{equation*}

    Thus the hoe uli is moving at an angular speed of \(50\pi\) radians per second.

  2. Since the radius is \(r=2\) ft and the angular speed is \(\omega=180\) is measure in radians per minute we can Definition 1.2.26 to calculate the linear speed

    \begin{equation*} v=r\omega=2\mbox{ft} \cdot50\pi\frac{\mbox{rad} }{\mbox{min} }\cdot\frac{\mbox{mile} }{5280\mbox{ft} }\cdot\frac{60\mbox{min} }{\mbox{hr} }\approx3.57\frac{\mbox{miles} }{\mbox{hour} } \end{equation*}

    Thus the steersperson moving the hoe uli at a linear speed of 3.57 mph.
